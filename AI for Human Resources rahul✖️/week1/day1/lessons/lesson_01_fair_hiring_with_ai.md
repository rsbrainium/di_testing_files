## Introduction

Every hiring process starts with a job description, and that one document does a lot of quiet work. It sets expectations, attracts (or repels) talent, and shapes the first impression people form of your organisation. Even experienced recruiters can let in phrasing that limits who feels welcome to apply, simply because we reuse the language we have always used.

This is where a generative AI assistant earns its place. A chat-based tool like Microsoft Copilot, ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini can draft a first version of a posting in minutes, scan your wording for biased or exclusionary phrasing, and suggest a salary range from market data. None of this replaces your judgment. Think of it as a very fast first-drafter and a second set of eyes that never gets tired of checking the same wording.

To make this concrete, the examples below all use the same role: hiring a **Data Analyst with 3–5 years of experience**. You will see the same role come back in the exercises, so you can compare what you produce with the lesson.

### 💼 Prerequisites

- You can write a request in plain English in a chat tool and read the reply
- You know what a job description and an interview look like in your organisation

## Why language in a job post matters

Language signals who belongs. Phrases like "digital native" can read as an age filter, and "culture fit" often rewards sameness rather than skill. Focusing on skills and outcomes keeps a posting open to a wider, more capable pool. An AI assistant can scan your draft for these signals automatically and offer neutral alternatives.

A small rewrite shows the effect:

| Version | Wording |
|---|---|
| Before | "Energetic professional ready to thrive in a fast-paced environment." |
| After | "Motivated professional eager to contribute to a collaborative, evolving team." |

![Hiring before AI versus with AI](images/before_after_hiring.png)
*Before AI, posting and screening were manual and inconsistent. With AI, the routine work shrinks so you can focus on people — as long as you keep checking the output.*

To draft a posting, you can prompt: *"Write a job description for a Data Analyst with 3–5 years of experience. Include key skills, responsibilities, and an inclusive tone."* The tool proposes sections, and you refine with follow-ups like *"Rewrite the qualifications to be more skill-based"* or *"Add language that emphasises flexible working options."* This iterative back-and-forth is the core skill — you are steering, not accepting the first draft.

> **Knowledge Check — Inclusive language**
>
> *Think about:* "Why might 'recent graduate, digital native, high-energy' narrow your applicant pool even though none of those words names a protected characteristic?"
>
> *Quick activity (2 min):* Open an AI tool and paste a real sentence from one of your job posts. Ask it to "flag any wording that could discourage qualified applicants and suggest neutral alternatives." Note one suggestion you agree with and one you would reject.

## From description to a fair interview

A strong posting is only the start. The next step in fair hiring is a structured rubric and bias-aware questions. A rubric is simply your hiring roadmap: it lists the skills you are looking for and describes what weak, adequate, and strong performance looks like, so every interviewer scores on the same basis.

Unstructured interviews lean on intuition and personal chemistry, which can reward confidence over competence. A shared rubric gives everyone the same questions and the same rating criteria. You can ask the AI to build this from your job description: *"Create a five-criteria hiring rubric scored 1–5,"* then *"Add examples of behaviours for each rating level."* You will likely get categories like collaboration, communication, and analytical reasoning, each with descriptions you can refine.

Once the rubric exists, generate questions from it: *"Generate open-ended interview questions for each skill in this rubric that avoid assumptions about background or personality."* The shift this produces is subtle but important — instead of "How do you handle stress?" you get "Tell me about a time you managed competing priorities under pressure." The second version asks for observable behaviour, which is fairer and easier to score.

> **Real-World Example — Unilever**
>
> Unilever rebuilt early-career hiring around AI-assisted screening and video interviews. Over roughly 18 months it reported saving more than 50,000 hours of candidate-interview time and over £1M in annual costs, while diversity of hires rose by about 16% and the process shrank from months to weeks.
>
> *Source: https://www.bestpractice.ai/ai-case-study-best-practice/unilever_saved_over_50,000_hours_in_candidate_interview_time_and_delivered_over_%C2%A31m_annual_savings_and_improved_candidate_diversity_with_machine_analysis_of_video-based_interviewing.*

> 📌 **Common Misconception**
>
> *"If the AI removed the biased words, the process is now fair."*
>
> **Reality:** AI works like spellcheck for fairness — it catches surface patterns, but it can also introduce new ones, and it cannot see your context. A posting can be perfectly worded and still screen people out through, say, an unnecessary degree requirement. The final review belongs to you.

## Salary benchmarks and saved prompts

Salary transparency builds trust. An AI tool can research benchmarks from sources like PayScale, Glassdoor, or LinkedIn and suggest a range based on role, location, and experience. Treat that number as a research shortcut, not a decision: always validate it against your internal pay framework, equity considerations, and any pay-transparency rules you must follow.

One practical habit pays off quickly. If you keep giving the AI the same instructions — your tone, your formatting, your compliance notes — most tools let you save these as custom instructions or reusable templates. Over time you build a small library of prompts that encode your organisation's voice, which makes your work faster and easier to share with colleagues.

## 🚀 Lesson Challenge

You are hiring the **Data Analyst (3–5 years)** for a mid-size company. In an AI tool of your choice:

**What to do:**
1. Draft the job description with one prompt, then improve it with at least two follow-up prompts (one for skill-based qualifications, one for inclusive tone).
2. Ask the tool to flag any wording that could discourage qualified applicants, and apply the changes you agree with.
3. Generate a 3-criteria scoring rubric (1–5) from your final description and one behaviour-based question per criterion.
