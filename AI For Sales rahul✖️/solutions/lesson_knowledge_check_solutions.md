## Lesson 1 — The AI Paradigm Shift in Sales

### Knowledge Check — Selling vs. non-selling time (after "Where your selling time actually goes")

**Question:** "Of the tasks you did yesterday, which were actual selling, and which were prep work an assistant could have started for you?"

**Answer:** Actual selling is live buyer interaction — calls, meetings, negotiation, relationship-building. Almost everything else (account research, CRM updates, reformatting decks, drafting emails) is prep work that an AI can at least begin. A typical answer finds that only one or two of yesterday's tasks were true selling, which mirrors the ~30% selling / ~70% non-selling split from the research.

### Knowledge Check — The three fits (after "The three places AI fits a sales workflow")

**Question:** "Which of the three — research, personalize, automate — would save you the most time this week?"

**Answer:** Any of the three is valid; the point is to name a *specific* task, not a category. A good answer is concrete, e.g., "Research — I'd hand over the first-pass account summary for my five new accounts, which usually costs me an hour each."

### Knowledge Check — The verify habit (after "Knowing what NOT to delegate")

**Question:** "What is the worst thing that could happen if you sent an AI-written claim to a buyer without checking it?"

**Answer:** You send a confident but wrong claim — an invented job title, a fake "recent" event, or an untrue competitor fact — and the buyer notices. That destroys credibility immediately and is hard to recover. The verification sentence should describe a real check, e.g., "I'll confirm every name and date against LinkedIn and the company site before it goes in an email."

### 🚀 Lesson Challenge

**What was asked:** Map five weekly tasks to Research / Personalize / Automate / Keep Human, then describe how you'd hand off your biggest time-sink.

**Example answer:**

| Task | Label |
|---|---|
| Research a new account | Research |
| Write follow-up emails | Automate |
| Tailor pitch for a new buyer | Personalize |
| Update CRM after calls | Automate |
| Negotiate contract terms | Keep Human |

> Biggest time-sink → "Research a new account. I'd ask: *'You're helping a rep selling freight software. Summarize [company]'s size, recent news, and likely ops challenges in a short brief — don't invent facts.'*"

## Lesson 2 — Coach the AI, Don't Just Ask It

### Knowledge Check — Spot the gap (after "Why vague prompts fail")

**Question:** "If you gave the prompt 'write a sales email' to five colleagues, how different would their results be — and why?"

**Answer:** Wildly different, because each colleague would fill the missing context with their own assumptions about the product, buyer, tone, and goal. That's exactly what the AI does — it fills gaps with the most average guess. Three things it must guess: who the buyer is, what you're selling, and what outcome the email should drive.

### Knowledge Check — Build a pillar (after "The three pillars of scaffolding")

**Question:** "Which pillar do you most often skip — context, instructions, or examples?"

**Answer:** Most people skip **examples**, because pasting a sample of past good work feels like extra effort. But examples are the fastest way to lock in tone. A strong context sentence looks like: "I sell a scheduling tool to dental clinics; my prospect went quiet after a demo last week."

### Knowledge Check — The anti-hallucination line (after "A reusable scaffolding recipe")

**Question:** "Why is 'don't invent facts, say so if unsure' more important in sales than in brainstorming blog titles?"

**Answer:** In brainstorming, a wrong guess costs nothing — you just discard it. In sales, an invented fact can land in a real message to a real buyer and damage trust or a deal. The stakes of a confident error are far higher, so the constraint is essential.

### 🚀 Lesson Challenge

**What was asked:** Rewrite "Write a follow-up email to a prospect" using the six-part recipe (dental scheduling tool, prospect went quiet after a demo).

**Example answer:**

> "**Role:** You're helping a B2B sales rep. **Context:** I sell a scheduling tool that cuts no-shows for dental clinics. My prospect, a practice manager, went quiet after a demo last week. **Task:** Write a short follow-up email re-opening the conversation. **Format/tone:** 80 words, warm and low-pressure, one clear next step. **Example:** match this past email I've sent: [paste]. **Constraint:** _Don't invent any features or stats — if unsure, leave it out._"

(The underlined constraint is the anti-hallucination line.)

## Lesson 3 — From Research to Personalized Outreach

### Knowledge Check — Focused questions (after "AI as your research buddy")

**Question:** "What three questions would you ask an AI to get up to speed on a brand-new account fast?"

**Answer:** Strong answers are specific and signal-oriented, e.g., (1) "What recent news or announcements mention this company?" (2) "What challenges typically face companies this size in this industry?" (3) "Who leads [the relevant function] here?" The goal is raw material to verify, not a finished verdict.

### Knowledge Check — Trigger to opener (after "Spotting trigger events")

**Question:** "Which trigger events would make a buyer in your market suddenly need what you sell?"

**Answer:** Depends on the market, but good examples are funding rounds, new executive hires, expansions/new locations, product launches, or regulatory changes — any event that creates the problem your product solves. A sample opener: "Congrats on the new fulfillment center — scaling sites is exactly when delivery visibility gets hard."

### Knowledge Check — Persona shift (after "Finding the right person — and personalizing the message")

**Question:** "How would your opening line change if you sent it to a CFO instead of a VP of Sales?"

**Answer:** The product stays the same; the value angle changes. To a CFO: lead with cost saved, risk reduced, payback. To a VP of Sales: lead with pipeline, faster ramp, predictable revenue. Example pair: "keep error costs flat as you scale" (CFO) vs. "ramp new reps to quota faster" (VP Sales).

### 🚀 Lesson Challenge

**What was asked:** Run the full research-to-outreach path: a trigger event, the decision-maker role + persona angle, and a two-sentence personalized opener.

**Example answer:**

> **Trigger:** Target opened a second warehouse. **Decision-maker:** VP of Operations (persona angle: fewer fire drills, visibility across sites). **Opener:** "Congrats on the second distribution center. Scaling to two sites usually makes delivery visibility harder right when accuracy matters most — we give ops teams one real-time view across warehouses so delays get caught before customers do."
