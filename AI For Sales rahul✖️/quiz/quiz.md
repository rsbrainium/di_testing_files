## Answer Key

**1. C) Reps spend only about a third of their week actually selling; most goes to non-selling prep**
Salesforce's research found reps spend roughly 70% of their time on non-selling work and about 30% selling. That gap is the opportunity AI addresses — by absorbing prep, not by replacing the seller. Options A and B contradict the data; D overstates current automation.

**2. B) Giving the AI a clear framework — context, instructions, and examples — instead of a vague request**
Scaffolding is exactly context + instructions + examples. A describes letting the AI lead (the opposite of providing structure), C confuses model choice with prompting, and D — shorter prompts — usually makes output worse.

**3. A) The prompt was vague, so the AI had to guess and produced an average result**
Generic output almost always traces back to a generic prompt: with gaps to fill, the AI picks the most average answer. The model isn't broken (B), AI can write strong emails when coached (C), and over-personalization (D) isn't what causes blandness.

**4. D) The company just announced a new funding round**
A trigger event is a meaningful change — funding, a hire, an expansion — that creates urgency. Having a website, employees, or a product (A, B, C) are permanent facts, not change signals.

**5. B) Treat the names as leads and verify each on LinkedIn or the company site before reaching out**
AI can hallucinate titles and people, so its output is a lead to verify, not a fact to act on. Emailing blindly (A) risks contacting the wrong person; ignoring it (C) wastes a useful starting point; asking the AI to "guarantee" accuracy (D) doesn't make it reliable.

**6. C) The value angle: cost and risk for the CFO, pipeline and ramp speed for the VP of Sales**
The product stays the same; the story changes per persona. Changing the product (A) makes no sense, "one message fits all" (B) is the mistake the lesson warns against, and changing only the greeting (D) isn't real personalization.

**7. A) "Don't invent facts — if you're unsure, say so and mark it unverified"**
This constraint directly targets hallucination, the biggest risk when AI output may reach a buyer. The others affect style or length but do nothing to prevent false claims.

**8. D) Trigger value fades over time and the first credible seller to act is far more likely to win**
Trigger-based outreach works best while the event is fresh; response rates drop the longer you wait, and the first seller to reach out after a trigger has a strong edge. A is false (funding is a classic trigger), B and C don't reflect how trigger timing works.

**9. B) AI can produce confident but wrong facts, so removing verification risks sending errors to buyers**
The course's central rule is "AI drafts and gathers; you verify and decide." Stripping out human checkpoints is exactly how a hallucinated title or stat ends up in a buyer's inbox. A, C, and D are all false about AI's capabilities.

**10. C) The fresh second-warehouse opening, because it's both highly relevant to the product and still timely**
The strongest trigger scores high on *both* relevance (it maps to what you sell) and freshness (the window is still open). The CEO quote is vague and low-relevance (A), the delivery delay is relevant but stale (B), and a brand-new job posting often means the owner isn't in seat yet (D).
