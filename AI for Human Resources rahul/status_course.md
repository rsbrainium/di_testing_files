# Course Status — AI for Human Resources

**Format**: MasterClass (3–4 hours)
**Total duration**: 1 session (~3.5 hours of guided content + practice)
**Target profile**: HR professionals (recruiters, HR managers, people-ops staff) with no coding background
**Code-based**: No (theoretical / applied — students work in chat-based AI tools, not code)

---

## Difficulty

| Level | Rating |
|---|---|
| **Overall difficulty** | 🟡 INTERMEDIATE |
| **Conceptual difficulty** | 🟡 INTERMEDIATE |
| **Technical difficulty** | 🟢 EASY |
| **Exercise difficulty** | 🟡 INTERMEDIATE |

### Assessment

For a non-technical HR audience, the hands-on mechanics of this MasterClass are genuinely easy: everything happens inside a chat-based assistant (Microsoft Copilot in the examples, but the same skills carry to ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini), so there is no software to install and nothing to code. What raises the rating to intermediate is the judgment layer, not the tools. Students have to hold two ideas at once — how to get useful output from AI *and* when not to trust it — across topics that carry real consequences: bias in hiring, candidate and employee privacy (PII, GDPR, CCPA), and the line between an assistant that drafts and a human who decides.

The difficulty is back-loaded. The opening blocks (writing fairer job descriptions, building interview rubrics) are concrete and immediately useful, so students gain confidence fast. The later material on AI agents — the shift from one-off prompting to a persistent, grounded assistant with defined boundaries — is the most abstract part and is where most HR learners will need to slow down. Compared with typical "intro to AI" courses, this one is lighter on theory and heavier on responsible use, which suits the audience but means the ethics and privacy threads run through every section rather than sitting in one tidy module.

## Prerequisites

Students should have the following before starting this MasterClass:

| # | Prerequisite | Required / Recommended | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Day-to-day HR experience (hiring, onboarding, or people ops) | Required | The exercises assume you recognise a job description, a rubric, and an engagement survey without explanation. |
| 2 | Comfort typing instructions into a chat tool and reading the reply | Required | The entire session is prompt-and-refine; you need to be at ease writing a request in plain English and iterating on it. |
| 3 | Basic familiarity with Word and Excel / Google Docs and Sheets | Recommended | AI tools read and write to these formats; knowing your way around a spreadsheet helps when analysing HR data. |
| 4 | Awareness of your organisation's data-privacy basics (what counts as confidential) | Recommended | Several exercises hinge on spotting when data should be anonymised before it goes near an AI tool. |

### What Students Do NOT Need

- **No coding, scripting, or technical background.** Every task is done by typing plain-English instructions into an AI assistant — if you can write an email, you can do this course.
- **No prior AI experience.** Prompting, grounding, and agents are all introduced from scratch with worked examples.
- **No specific software licence.** The examples use Microsoft Copilot, but the methods transfer to any major AI assistant, so you can follow along with whichever tool your organisation has approved.

## Session Overview

This MasterClass runs as a single session built from three connected blocks. Each block pairs a short lesson with hands-on practice in an AI tool.

| Block | Main Topic | Key Concepts | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|---|
| Block 1 | Fairer hiring with AI prompts | Inclusive job descriptions, bias-aware language, interview rubrics and behaviour-based questions, salary benchmarking | 🟢 EASY |
| Block 2 | AI for HR data & documents | Working with files (grounding), workforce-skill forecasting, compliance-document review, engagement-survey analysis, privacy and PII | 🟡 INTERMEDIATE |
| Block 3 | From prompts to agents | When to move from one-off prompts to a persistent agent, agent design (purpose, instructions, grounding, boundaries), employee-facing risk, monitoring after launch | 🟡 INTERMEDIATE |

A short capstone ties the three blocks together: choosing the right AI approach for a given HR task and defending the choice on grounds of fairness, privacy, and human accountability.

## Notes for Instructors

The single most common stumbling point is the prompt-to-agent shift in Block 3. HR learners readily understand "ask the AI to draft something," but the idea of a *scoped, grounded assistant that knows when to stop and hand off to a human* is genuinely new — budget extra time and lean on the parental-leave-question example from the source material. Expect privacy anxiety to surface early; address it head-on in Block 1 rather than deferring it, because students will hesitate to paste anything into a tool until they trust the boundary between placeholder data and real PII. Finally, keep reinforcing the through-line that runs across all three blocks: AI drafts, surfaces patterns, and suggests — but the HR professional checks for bias, protects people's data, and owns the final decision.

### Currency note

Figures and tool capabilities cited in the course reflect mid-2026: roughly 39–43% of organisations now use AI within HR (up from about 26% a year earlier), with the heaviest use in recruiting, and large majorities of adopters reporting efficiency and work-quality gains. Microsoft Copilot's current generation can open and analyse PDFs and spreadsheets directly in chat and run multi-step tasks across Microsoft 365 apps. Verify these against a fresh source before each delivery, since both adoption numbers and tool features move quickly.
