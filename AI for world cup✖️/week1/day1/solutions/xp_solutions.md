## Exercise 1 — Spot and replace exclusionary wording

### Question 1 — Flagged phrases and replacements

**Answer:**

The draft is full of wording that signals age, energy, and sameness rather than skill. A strong submission flags phrases like these (other reasonable flags are acceptable):

| # | Flagged phrase | Why it could exclude | Neutral replacement |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "energetic, high-energy" | Reads as an age/ability signal; penalises anyone who works calmly or has a disability | "Able to manage several priorities effectively" |
| 2 | "digital native" | Proxy for age — implies only younger people qualify | "Comfortable working with data tools and software" |
| 3 | "recent graduate" | Screens out experienced career-changers and older applicants | "Has 3–5 years of relevant analytical experience" |
| 4 | "culture fit" | Rewards sameness; invites unconscious bias | "Works well in a collaborative team" |
| 5 | "young, dynamic self-starter" | "Young" is an explicit age signal | "Self-directed and proactive" |
| 6 | "thrive under pressure / fast-paced" | Can deter people with caregiving needs or disabilities | "Comfortable working to deadlines" |

**Why this is correct:** none of the original phrases names a protected characteristic, which is exactly why they slip through — they act as *proxies* for age, ability, or background. Replacing them with skill- and behaviour-based wording keeps the bar high while widening who feels invited to apply.

## Exercise 2 — Rewrite the posting and build a rubric

### Part A — Rewritten posting (excerpt)

**Answer:**

A good rewrite keeps the role demanding but skill-focused, for example:

> *"Brightline Logistics is hiring a Data Analyst (3–5 years' experience) to turn delivery and route data into clear recommendations. You'll build reports, spot trends, and work closely with operations. We value people who manage multiple priorities effectively, communicate findings simply, and are comfortable with data tools. We offer flexible working options and support your growth."*

### Part B — Rubric and questions

**Answer:**

| Criterion | Weak (1–2) | Adequate (3) | Strong (4–5) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Analytical reasoning | Describes tasks, not analysis; misses obvious patterns | Identifies clear trends with prompting | Frames the right question, tests it, and explains trade-offs |
| 2. Communication | Jargon-heavy; loses a non-technical listener | Explains results clearly when asked | Tailors the message to the audience and recommends next steps |
| 3. Collaboration | Works in isolation; little stakeholder contact | Responds to requests from other teams | Proactively partners with operations to shape the analysis |

**Three behaviour-based questions (one per criterion):**
1. "Tell me about a time you used data to challenge an assumption your team held. What did you find and what did you do?"
2. "Describe a moment you had to explain a complex finding to someone non-technical. How did you approach it?"
3. "Walk me through a project where you worked with another team to get the data you needed."

**Why this is correct:** the rubric gives every interviewer the same scale and the same evidence of "strong," and the questions ask for *observable behaviour* ("tell me about a time…") rather than self-assessment ("are you good at…"), which is the fairness principle from the lesson.
