# 💡 Daily Digest — The AI Recruiter That Taught Itself to Reject Women

![How biased training data produced a biased recruiting tool](images/digest_01_amazon_bias.png)
*A model trained on a decade of mostly-male résumés learned to penalise the word "women's" — and was ultimately scrapped.*

---

## The Hook

One of the world's most sophisticated tech companies built an AI to screen résumés — and quietly killed it when it discovered the tool had taught itself that being a woman was a negative.

---

## The Story

Between 2014 and 2017, Amazon developed an experimental machine-learning tool to score job applicants, the way a shopper rates products from one to five stars. The promise was obvious: feed in a pile of CVs, get back a ranked shortlist, save recruiters hundreds of hours.

There was a catch hiding in the data. The model was trained on roughly ten years of résumés the company had received — and in tech, those came overwhelmingly from men. The system did exactly what it was built to do: it looked for patterns in past "successful" candidates and learned to favour people who resembled them. In practice, that meant it started penalising résumés that contained the word "women's" — as in "women's chess club captain" — and reportedly downgraded graduates of two all-women's colleges.

Amazon's engineers tried to edit the tool to ignore those specific terms. But that only fixed the symptoms they could see; there was no guarantee the model wasn't finding other, subtler proxies for gender. The company couldn't be confident the tool would ever be reliably fair, so it scrapped the project.

The episode became the textbook example of a simple, uncomfortable truth: **AI learns from the past, and the past is not neutral.** A tool that simply reproduces who got hired before will reproduce yesterday's blind spots at machine speed and machine scale.

| | Manual screening | Biased AI screening |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Slow, inconsistent | Fast, consistent |
| Bias | Present but case-by-case | Systematic and scaled up |
| Visibility of bias | Hard to see | Hidden inside the model |
| Outcome at Amazon | — | Project scrapped |

---

## Why This Matters for You

This is the exact reason today's lessons keep returning to one phrase: AI is a second set of eyes, not the decision-maker. The same tools that draft fairer job descriptions can also bake in old bias if you point them at the wrong data and stop paying attention. The skill isn't avoiding AI — it's staying in the loop, checking its output for fairness, and remembering that "the computer ranked them" is never a defence.
